Risks of Bad Password Management

The most common example of Bad Password Management.

Posted by Anton Talalaev on November 28, 2020

Information technology development has led to the creation of a whole online world with specific rules of communication, safety rules, and a large number of personal accounts. An ordinary person can have well over 5 different personal accounts with personal information. To access their account, a person has to “log in”, which means entering a username and password. It is really hard to memorize login information for all existing accounts. To make it easier, quite often, people choose to use one password for all accounts.

Even though one password is much easier to memorize, this method of password management has some critical negative effects.

This article will cover the very real consequences
of having one password for all accounts

Now just imagine that cybercriminals hack into one of the Internet sites on which you have a personal account. Then your personal data, such as phone number, date of birth, mailing address, password to the hacked account, etc. go to a special dataset of hacked accounts. Such databases are themselves used as standalone black market goods. But the most dangerous is that such databases are used by malefactors to hack other online services and payment systems.

In this case, if you've used the same password for all of your accounts. One hacked website, where you had an account, puts all your other accounts at risk. You can lose access to your social accounts, email, and other accounts. If you didn't set recoverying setting, some accounts that were stolen, you will never be able to return or restore.

For example, cybercriminals can steal your PlayStation game account, where all games that were purchased online in the digital version. In the case of a stolen social account, all friends of yours can receive messages from you where they will be told that you need a small amount of money urgently, and a mobile phone or a bank account to transfer the asked amount.

In conclusion, I would like to give you a shortlist of useful pieces of advice on how to protect yourself from getting hacked:

  • Don’t use the same password for multiple accounts.
  • Don’t use the same password for multiple accounts. The reason for that I already described above.
  • Some online services provide you an opportunity to “sign-in” using linked accounts Facebook/Google/Other. Don’t use a Facebook account or any other account to create accounts that you are not ready to lose (only for one-time services).
  • Always use two-factor authentication where it is possible. Nowadays, this is the best way to keep an account secure.
  • Always set a recovery setting everywhere where it is possible. Such settings usually include trusted email addresses, mobile phones, and security questions. Such settings are the only way to restore your account in case it will be stolen.
